## Acquisition vs refinance: decision context

Acquisition and refinance financing share the same core underwriting metrics—DSCR, debt yield, and leverage—but they answer different sponsor questions. Acquisitions fund a new basis and business plan. Refinances reset capital structure on an existing asset, often to return equity, extend maturity, or improve terms.

Use this framework to align product selection, documentation, and timeline expectations before engaging lenders.

## Underwriting focus differences

### Acquisition financing

- **Valuation** is forward-looking: purchase price, business plan, and stabilization timeline drive proceeds.
- **Diligence** includes PSA contingencies, seller documents, and third-party reports tied to closing date.
- **Product fit** spans [bridge](/loan-types/bridge-value-add/) for transitional deals and [agency](/loan-types/agency-stabilized/) or [CMBS](/loan-types/cmbs-stabilized/) for stabilized acquisitions.

### Refinance financing

- **Valuation** emphasizes proven operations: trailing NOI, seasoning, and appraisal support for current performance.
- **Diligence** includes existing loan payoff, prepayment estimate, reserve reconciliation, and historical lender reporting.
- **Cash-out** adds use-of-proceeds scrutiny and post-close covenant tests ([cash-out guide](/learn/multifamily-cash-out-refinance/)).

## Documentation and timeline

| Workstream | Acquisition | Refinance |
|------------|-------------|-----------|
| Financial package | Pro forma + T12 if available | T12, YTD, historical reporting |
| Third-party reports | Often tied to PSA timeline | May update prior reports; appraisal refresh |
| Legal | Purchase contract, title, survey | Payoff, existing loan docs, entity continuity |
| Sponsor focus | Business plan credibility | Operating stability and covenant history |

Refinances can move quickly when the asset is stabilized and the [lender document package](/checklists/lender-document-checklist/) is complete. Acquisitions depend on PSA milestones and seller cooperation.

## Product selection patterns

**Stabilized acquisition** — Agency or CMBS permanent debt when in-place NOI supports proceeds at closing.

**Value-add acquisition** — Bridge with documented capex scope, reserve plan, and [refinance exit map](/learn/rate-risk-and-refinance-planning/).

**Rate-and-term refinance** — Replace existing debt without pulling equity; compare prepayment cost versus long-term savings.

**Cash-out refinance** — Return equity after stabilization; size from normalized NOI and binding constraints.

**Recapitalization near maturity** — Start 12–18 months before maturity; compare extension, refinance, and sale paths.

## Risk factors by path

### Acquisition risks

- Overpaying relative to stabilized NOI.
- Underestimating capex and lease-up timeline on bridge paths.
- Selecting permanent debt before the asset is truly stabilized.

### Refinance risks

- Prepayment penalties that consume expected proceeds.
- Appraisal shortfalls versus sponsor basis expectations.
- Thin seasoning or weak T12 support for requested cash-out.
- Entity or ownership changes that trigger lender re-review.

## Practical comparison checklist

1. Define sponsor objective: new basis, return equity, extend maturity, or reduce rate.
2. Build in-place NOI view with documented normalization.
3. Size debt under DSCR, debt yield, and LTV for the appropriate path.
4. For refinances, model payoff and prepayment cost before comparing quotes.
5. Match product to asset stage—bridge for transition, permanent for stabilization.
6. Prepare the correct document package for acquisition or refinance submission.

This comparison is educational and should be adapted with transaction-specific financing, legal, tax, and accounting advice.
